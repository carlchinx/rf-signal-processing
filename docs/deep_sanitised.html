Solid lines: S21 insertion loss (left axis). Dashed lines: group delay (right axis), the negative derivative of unwrapped S21 phase. Resonator Structure & Phase Distortion
Resonator Pole Map derivedLoaded Q extracted from GD peaks · Q = π·f₀·τ_peak
Each circle is a resonator pole extracted from a group delay peak. Position = resonant frequency; size = loaded Q factor (QL = π·f₀·GDpeak). Larger circles are higher-Q (sharper, less lossy) resonators. The tight vertical clustering at 3.7 and 4.3 GHz confirms these are coupled-resonator pairs forming the filter. Spread between units = manufacturing detuning of individual cavities.
Phase Linearity — Residuals derivedDeviation from best-fit linear phase in passband (deg)
A perfectly linear phase response (constant group delay) passes all frequencies with equal delay, preserving waveform shape. Shown here: the residual after subtracting the best-fit straight line from the unwrapped S21 phase within the passband. The ±40–45° swing across the band is the phase distortion any signal would incur — relevant for any modulated waveform or pulsed radar application.
Signal Integrity & Passivity
Gaussian Pulse Propagation simulated600 MHz-wide pulse centred at 4 GHz · IFFT convolution
Input: a Gaussian RF pulse centred at 4 GHz, bandwidth ≈600 MHz (white). Output per unit: the pulse after multiplication by the complex S21 in frequency domain, then IFFT. The differential delays (2.9–3.3 ns) and amplitude variation across units directly predict how a pulsed radar or communication burst would behave. Unit Unit 4 shows the most distortion and broadening consistent with its offset centre frequency.
Power Partition passivityTransmitted + Reflected + Dissipated = 1 W/W at every frequency
Every watt into the filter must appear as: transmitted (useful signal), reflected (bounced back at the input), or dissipated (heat). Stopband power is almost entirely reflected — the filter is reactive, not absorptive. The ~13% dissipation floor in the passband is the conductor and dielectric loss budget of the physical resonators. Shaded region shows spread across visible units.

Top: PC1 (amber, 92% of variance) and PC2 (sky, 5%) as functions of frequency — the dominant spatial patterns of unit-to-unit variation. PC1 is an antisymmetric frequency-shift mode: units shift the entire passband up or down. PC2 is a bandwidth-ripple mode: passband shape changes independently of centre. Stopband Rejection at Wireless BandsS21 interpolated at key interference frequencies (dBc)
Rejection at frequencies relevant to coexistence: 700–2400 MHz cellular bands (lower stopband), and 5–10 GHz Wi-Fi/radar bands (upper stopband). All units exceed 100 dB rejection below 2.4 GHz. Upper stopband rejection degrades above 7 GHz — the filter's harmonic window where the periodic resonator structure allows partial transmission.
Impedance & Dispersive Properties
S11 Impedance Locus — Smith ChartInput reflection · passband highlighted · outer rim = total reflection
The locus hugs the outer rim (|Γ|=1, total reflection) in the stopband. In the passband it spirals inward toward the chart centre (matched, −∞ dB return loss). The tightness of the four units' spirals confirms consistent impedance matching. The inward spiral direction (clockwise) confirms a resonant/bandpass topology vs a purely reactive structure.
Electrical Length Dispersion derivedPhase transit time normalised to frequency · ns · passband 3–5.5 GHz
Electrical length θ/ω = −∠S21/(2πf) gives the average phase transit time from DC to frequency f — a measure of dispersiveness. A non-dispersive medium would show a flat horizontal line. The monotonic slope reveals the filter is strongly dispersive through the passband: higher frequencies travel faster through the structure than lower ones (anomalous dispersion), consistent with a coupled-resonator topology.
